Situation

Prospecting a food retailer is not a templated email job.

Every retailer has its own history, store network, leadership, strategy, regulatory context and installed competitors. And every contact inside a retailer reads something completely different: someone from Sales will react to financial arguments (margin recovery, shrinkage cost, incremental revenue), while someone from Operations will care about time saved in store and consistent processes across the whole network.

To do this properly for a single account, the work is roughly:

  1. Research the retailer (financials, press, leadership, store network, sustainability commitments)
  2. Segment 10 to 30 contacts (sometimes more) into 3 to 5 persona groups
  3. Write 3 to 5 tailored sequences with the right angle, the right case study, the right tone, the right call to action
  4. Apply 20+ editorial rules that protect Smartway's voice (no em dashes like this "—", Spain Spanish only, one argument per email, and so on)
  5. Double-check every figure used in copy

Done by hand, one full account takes a focused BDR around 8 hours of work.

Task

Codify 12 months of learnings into a reusable AI skill.

The goal was simple: stop re-explaining context to the AI every time. Build a single reference document that captures how I prospect for Smartway, what I say to whom, what I never say, and which case studies fit which conversation. Then plug it into Claude so it loads automatically every time.

Action

I built a "skill": a structured playbook the AI reads before every task.

Concretely, this is a reference document Claude opens automatically whenever I start working on a sequence. It contains four stages and every standard I have learned in more than one year of prospecting Spanish and French-speaking food retailers.

1

Account research

The exact sources to check, the data points to gather, the red flags to avoid. Plus the latest news and stakeholder interviews that reinforce our message on FWMS or FOS, turning a cold email into a direct echo of what the prospect said publicly.

2

Contact segmentation

How to group 10 to 30 contacts into 3 to 5 persona sequences instead of writing one sequence per person.

3

Sequence structure

The exact step-by-step structure of a multi-channel sequence (emails, LinkedIn messages, calls), per persona, per solution.

4

Editorial standards

Every rule that defines my voice when prospecting for Smartway: tone, length, vocabulary, case studies to use.

What the skill brings, beyond speed

  • Sequences that complement each other. Within one account, the IT, Sales, Operations and Finance sequences use different angles but tell a coherent story. A contact who shares them with a colleague sees a consistent Smartway, not three unrelated emails.
  • Knowledge that builds up. Every learning from a new account (a good subject line, a new objection handled, a new case study angle) gets added to the skill. The next prospect benefits from everything that came before.
  • Pick up where I left off. If I need to revive an account 3 months later, the skill remembers the angle, the contacts, the language used. No starting from scratch.
  • Built for multiple markets. Spain, France, Belgium, Luxembourg, Morocco, Polynesia. Each has its own language, register and reference accounts. The skill applies the right one without asking.
Why it matters

A skill is not a one-off. It is a method that can be applied to any repetitive workflow a BDR (or anyone else) faces. What worked for sequences can work for account research, contact research, CRM notes.

What's next

Make it sharper, make it shared, make it scale.

  • Take the skill to the next level. Document each customer case with its key metrics, capture the angles and approaches that worked best, and turn the running list of learnings into a structured library.
  • Share it with anyone who wants it. Any colleague who wants to adapt the method to their own scope is welcome to use it as a starting point. The goal is to give them a head start, not impose a template.
  • Replicate the approach on daily tasks. The same logic can be applied to recurring micro-workflows: meeting recaps, follow-up notes, contact research. Small repetitive tasks where consistency matters more than creativity.
